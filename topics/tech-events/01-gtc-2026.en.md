## 1. Vera Rubin Platform: A Heterogeneous Architecture for Agentic AI

### 1.1 Roadmap and the Bigger Picture: From Blackwell to Vera Rubin

If you only look at product names, Vera Rubin can feel like “just the next chip after Blackwell.” But from an architecture perspective, it’s really the next phase in a longer roadmap.

Roughly speaking, you can group the recent generations like this:

- **Blackwell (2024–2025)**  
  - B200 / GB200 as the flagship GPU platform  
  - Typical rack power already creeping toward 120kW+, close to the limits of many existing data centers
- **Vera Rubin (late 2026)**  
  - Extends compute beyond Blackwell‑class performance  
  - More importantly: **redesigns the division of labor** between CPU, GPU, memory, and interconnect  
  - Promotes inference control and data movement to first‑class design goals
- **Feynman (2027+)**  
  - Longer‑term roadmap node  
  - Relevant here only as a hint of where Physical AI and digital twins might be headed

To make that shift more concrete, it helps to compress the roadmap into a simple table:

| Time        | Platform   | What changes focus                                    |
|-------------|------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| 2024–2025   | Blackwell  | Push GPU racks to the limits of existing facilities  |
| 2026 (late) | Vera Rubin | Turn GPU + CPU + interconnect into a single platform |
| 2027+       | Feynman    | Longer‑term architectures for Physical AI / twins    |

You can also slice it as three **workload eras** rather than three product names:

| Era         | Primary applications / business model          | Workload fingerprint                          | Architectural focus                         |
|-------------|-----------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| Blackwell   | Giant‑model training + early online inference | Long batch training + first‑generation online | “Make a single GPU rack as fast as you can”|
| Vera Rubin  | Large‑scale online inference + agentic AI     | High concurrency, long context, agentic loops | “Promote inference control to a first‑class citizen” |
| Feynman     | Physical AI / digital twins                   | Real‑world feedback loops, safety‑critical    | “Extend from the data center into the physical world” |

From a system‑design point of view, something important is happening:

> **NVIDIA is no longer just stacking bigger GPUs. It’s redefining who “thinks”, who “calculates”, and who “moves data” inside the system.**

In previous generations, the “design knobs” for the system lived mostly on the GPU:

- More SMs and Tensor Cores  
- More HBM channels and bandwidth  
- Push the Roofline higher and hope the rest of the system keeps up

By the time we get to Vera Rubin, the pressure from inference has changed the game:

- **Cost curve** – how do you sustain 24×7 inference under power and TCO constraints? (Little’s Law + queuing theory)
- **Agentic behavior** – when hundreds or thousands of agents collaborate on long‑running tasks, who maintains their state and coordination? (Amdahl + USL)
- **Long‑context, multi‑stage pipelines** – when workloads shift from single Q&A to long‑lived workflows, where do the bottlenecks actually move? (Roofline)

In short, we’re moving from *processor‑centric* design to *AI‑factory‑centric* design. In that regime, **orchestrator efficiency and data movement cost often hit you before raw FLOPs do**, and that’s exactly where Vera CPU enters the picture.

### 1.2 Vera CPU: Deterministic Orchestration for Agentic Workloads

In a traditional heterogeneous system, the CPU’s job description is simple:

- Run the OS and runtime
- Schedule work and coordinate IO
- Offload heavy matrix math to the GPU

That model worked fine in the **offline training** world, where latency budgets were loose and throughput and cost dominated the discussion. In the world of **agentic AI** and large‑scale inference, that old role starts to break down:

- Many agents run concurrently, calling tools and services
- Each agent needs its own context and long‑term state
- The system has to make decisions, execute actions, and update world state on a millisecond‑to‑second timescale

In that setting, the CPU stops being a “background manager” and becomes the **reasoning / orchestration engine** of the system:

- It decides which agent runs now and which waits
- It decides which tasks go to the GPU and which stay local
- It determines when to wait on IO and when to push ahead speculatively

If this orchestration layer has unpredictable latency, the entire system becomes hard to reason about and nearly impossible to give tight SLOs for. That’s why Vera is positioned as a CPU with **deterministic execution** as a first‑class goal.

One way to reason about this is through a microarchitectural reading of Little’s Law:

> **In‑flight ≈ IPC × Latency**

If you aggressively crank up IPC, but your memory system and branch behavior are noisy, you don’t just get 
“fast on average.” You get **large swings in latency**:

- Every L3 miss
- Every TLB miss
- Every misprediction

…stretches a much larger in‑flight window. That might be acceptable when you’re grinding through a long training job. It’s devastating when you’re on the critical path of an agentic workflow, where a handful of outliers can dominate the user experience.

Vera’s design leans in the opposite direction:

- Push IPC and width where it makes sense
- But put just as much emphasis on **predictable, bounded latency**
- So the CPU behaves like a stable, low‑jitter traffic controller for the rest of the system

If we zoom out to Arm’s 2026 product stack, you can roughly divide the cores into two worlds:

- **Cortex** – for phones, laptops, and consumer SoCs (X‑series big cores, A‑series medium/small cores), tuned for user‑perceived burst performance.
- **Neoverse** – for data centers and HPC (N/V series), tuned for 24×7 throughput and scalability.

They mostly share the same **Armv9‑A ISA**—what your program sees—but differ a lot in microarchitectural knobs:

- **Decode width** – how many instructions you can “understand” per cycle
- **Reorder buffer (ROB) / in‑flight capacity** – how many instructions you keep in flight (`In‑Flight = IPC × Latency`)
- **Vector engine (SVE2) width** – how many data elements you touch per instruction
- **Cache / L2 / L3 size and latency** – whether you can sustain large, pointer‑chasing working sets

Putting public information from Arm whitepapers and Hot Chips talks into a **conceptual comparison** (order‑of‑magnitude, not spec‑sheet numbers) gives something like:

| Feature      | Cortex‑X925 (mobile big core) | Cortex‑A725 (high‑efficiency big core) | Neoverse V3 (server / HPC)                  |
|-------------|--------------------------------|----------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Design goal | Peak single‑thread burst IPC   | Perf/Watt balance                      | Data‑center throughput / scalability        |
| Decode width| ~10‑wide                       | ~4–5‑wide                              | ~6–8‑wide                                   |
| ROB size    | ~768+ entries                  | Medium (few hundreds)                  | Large (≈300–400)                            |
| L2 / L3     | Medium, tuned for single core  | Smaller, area‑efficient                | Large, low‑latency, shared                  |
| Vector engine | SVE2, mostly 128‑bit usage   | SVE2, efficiency‑oriented             | SVE2, 256/512‑bit, aimed at HPC / AI        |
| Scaling     | 1–2 big cores + many small     | 4–8 cores per SoC                     | 64–128+ cores, CXL / PCIe 6 connectivity    |

From this angle, you can think of:

- X925 as the “supercar engine” in a flagship phone—great for short bursts.
- A725 as the “marathon runner” core—balanced for long‑term Perf/Watt.
- Neoverse V3 as the “industrial motor” in the data center—built for 24×7 throughput and TB/s‑class memory systems.

According to GTC 2026 session **[CWES81771]**, Vera CPU uses NVIDIA’s own Arm‑based custom microarchitecture in the **Neoverse‑V‑class** family, not a recycled mobile X‑core. The tuning is pointed squarely at **agentic inference loops**:

- It doesn’t chase X925‑style extreme 10‑wide decode; instead it balances somewhere around **6–8‑wide with a large ROB**—enough ILP/MLP without blowing up per‑core power.
- It spends more transistor budget on **L2/L3, prefetchers, and the memory hierarchy**, in line with the Memory Wall / Roofline story from *Computer Architecture 02*.
- It strengthens **SVE2 and CXL 3.0 / PCIe Gen 6** connectivity so the CPU can act as an efficient host for GPU / DPU / NPU complexes.

Put differently:

> **Vera CPU is not “a bigger mobile big core.” It’s a Neoverse‑class custom design retuned for data‑center agentic workloads.**

That design philosophy also explains why media shorthand like “up to 22,500 concurrent agents” keeps showing up: the point is not single‑core peak IPC; it’s the platform’s ability to host **large numbers of concurrent, tool‑calling agents** without the orchestrator turning into a queuing black hole.

In recent years, this sits in interesting contrast with **LPU‑style (Language Processing Unit) designs**:

- **LPU architectures** lean on huge on‑chip SRAM for ultra‑low latency, great for **small models + massive concurrency** on a fixed topology.
- **Vera CPU + Rubin GPU** take a more system‑level stance:
  - Use HBM4 and rack‑scale interconnect to support large models and long context.
  - Use the CPU to provide stable, predictable control flow and data movement—the “central nervous system” for agentic systems.

You can summarize the trade‑offs in a simple table (conceptual, not product spec):

| Design            | Latency  | Memory capacity           | Topology flexibility         |
|-------------------|----------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| LPU (SRAM‑heavy)  | Ultra‑low| Limited to a single die   | Mostly scale‑up              |
| Vera + Rubin      | Low, predictable | HBM4 + rack‑scale memory | Large‑scale scale‑out      |

The engineering target NVIDIA hints at for Vera Rubin is roughly:

> **Keep per‑token latency in the sub‑100ms regime for very large models and long context, while preserving enough determinism that the overall agentic system remains designable and debuggable.**

In other words, Vera is not just “a faster host CPU.” It’s **a deterministic orchestrator for agentic workloads**, tuned at the microarchitectural level for exactly that job.

### 1.3 Rubin GPU and the Roofline View of Inference

On the GPU side, Rubin is a natural successor to Blackwell, but GTC 2026 makes the **Roofline model** feel almost like part of the product spec.

If you sketch Roofline on a whiteboard:

- The x‑axis is **Arithmetic Intensity (AI)** – how much useful compute you do per byte moved
- The y‑axis is achievable FLOPs
- The slanted line is your memory‑bound region; the flat line is your compute ceiling

At a very rough bandwidth level, Rubin vs Blackwell look something like this (order‑of‑magnitude, not official spec):

| Item                    | Blackwell (HBM3e / NVLink 5) | Rubin (HBM4 / NVLink 6) |
|-------------------------|------------------------------|-------------------------|
| HBM memory bandwidth    | ~8 TB/s                      | ~22 TB/s                |
| NVLink GPU↔GPU bandwidth| ~1.8 TB/s                    | ~3.6 TB/s               |

The exact numbers will shift with real products; what matters is the **scale**. When per‑GPU HBM and on‑package interconnect bandwidth move up by multiples, the bottleneck naturally drifts from “single‑card FLOPs” toward **cross‑GPU, cross‑rack data movement and topology design**.

We can roughly place two main phases of LLM inference on this diagram:

- **Prefill** – typically **memory‑bound**  
  - Pulling large amounts of weights and KV cache through the memory hierarchy  
  - You ride the diagonal, limited by bandwidth
- **Decode** – often more **latency / control‑bound**  
  - Shorter, more irregular kernels  
  - Sensitive to tail latency and jitter

To make this concrete, we often use:

> **Arithmetic Intensity (AI) ≈ FLOPs / Bytes**  
> (How much useful work you get done for each byte you move.)

Rubin + HBM4 push both axes upward:

- More raw FLOPs at the top of the chart
- More bandwidth and capacity on the memory side

But the real story is not just “more of everything.” It’s the deliberate attempt to **shift workloads to the right** on the AI axis—by shrinking the number of bytes needed per unit of useful compute.

### 1.4 Numerical Formats: Hacking the Memory Wall

This is where the new **numerical and model‑level tricks** come in: NVFP4, 1.58‑bit quantization, and sparse attention kernels.

Roughly:

- **NVFP4 (4‑bit floating point)** – squeezes activations and sometimes weights into 4 bits while preserving useful dynamic range, so every cache line and HBM transaction carries **2×+** the semantic payload per byte.
- **1.58‑bit quantization (BitNet‑style)** – collapses weights into a ternary `{−1, 0, 1}` space, dramatically shrinking parameter storage and access costs, especially attractive for edge and embedded deployments.
- **Sparse attention kernels** – for very long contexts (think 128k tokens and beyond), prune most of the attention matrix so VRAM and bandwidth scale sub‑linearly with sequence length instead of exploding.

These sound like “ML tricks,” but architecturally they attack two very concrete problems:

- In the data center, **inference cost is often driven by “bytes per token” more than peak FLOPs**. Shrinking formats cuts how much you need to move for each token.
- On robots, vehicles, and other edge systems, **power and memory budgets are far below data‑center levels**. Aggressive quantization is often the only way to fit useful models into the available envelope.

In *Computer Architecture 04*, we usually express this with the Roofline vocabulary:

> **Arithmetic Intensity (AI) ≈ FLOPs / Bytes**

Every time you push weights from 16‑bit to 8‑bit to 4‑bit, or down into 1.58‑bit territory, you’re effectively doing two things at once:

- On the data‑center side, the same HBM / NVLink bandwidth can support more useful FLOPs per second, so your workloads move **to the right** on the AI axis and can rise closer to the compute ceiling—brute‑forcing your way over the traditional **memory wall**.
- On the edge / robotics side, you’re representing the same information with fewer bits, “flattening” memory and bandwidth pressure in exchange for a bit more arithmetic and model‑design complexity.

On the Roofline chart, this is exactly **shoving the workload to the right**: you’re doing the same math for fewer bytes, moving from the memory‑bound diagonal toward the compute‑bound plateau.

In that sense, low‑precision and sparse formats are less of a niche ML curiosity and more of an architectural lever:

> **They are a hacker’s way of bending the memory‑bound diagonal, turning bandwidth into effective compute.**

Rubin, HBM4, and these formats are best understood as a package, not three loosely related buzzwords.

### 1.5 Power, Cooling, and CPO: When Thermals Become Part of the Architecture

So far, we’ve been talking at the logical level: IPC, Roofline, numerical formats. GTC 2026 forces us to follow the chain all the way down into **power and cooling**.

At Rubin‑class densities, you don’t just 
“add more GPUs” to a rack. You ask:

- At 100–120kW per rack, what does my **power delivery** look like?
- How do I keep everything inside a **tight thermal envelope** without blowing up PUE or floor space?
- What does this do to my **networking layer**, especially optics?

This is where 800V bus bars, **direct liquid cooling**, and **co‑packaged optics (CPO)** stop being optional and start becoming **preconditions** for building AI factories.

Modern CPO designs often rely on **Micro Ring Modulators (MRMs)**. They’re remarkable:

- Extremely high bandwidth density
- Excellent energy efficiency per bit

…and also **extremely temperature‑sensitive**.

A rough mental model:

- Think of each MRM as a tiny tuning fork, locked to a specific resonance wavelength.
- Nudge the temperature outside a very small window, and:
  - Losses spike
  - Eye diagrams collapse
  - You’re forced to compensate, slow down, or retransmit

This has two important consequences:

1. **Rack‑level power and cooling design directly affect network stability.**  
   It’s not just about keeping GPUs under their TJ limits. You have to keep the CPO and MRMs inside a very narrow thermal band, or your fabric starts injecting jitter and retransmits into the system.
2. You end up needing **precise thermal control loops** at the rack / pod level.  
   The thermal control system effectively becomes part of the architecture. It dictates how aggressive you can be with rack density, and how stable your network latency distribution really is.

This is our first glimpse of a recurring theme in this article:

> **As you move toward AI Factories and Physical AI, details that look like “packaging” or “thermal design” start showing up in your latency and reliability graphs.**

With Vera CPU, Rubin GPU, HBM4, CPO, and thermal control loops all on the table, we’ve set the stage for the next question: if training used to be the star, what happens when **inference** becomes the main business?

That’s where we turn next.

## 2. Disaggregated Inference: From Training to Production Architecture

### 2.1 Why Inference Is a Trillion‑Dollar Question

Earlier that week, Chen had pointed at a rising cost curve on the lab display.

“We only train the model once,” he said. “So why is the bill going up every month?”

Yang had already pulled the request logs: more than **90% of the GPU spend** was coming from long‑running, always‑on inference workloads, not one‑off training jobs.

Most of the early excitement around large models focused on **training**: bigger clusters, more tokens, longer context. But if you look at how AI shows up in real products, **inference** is where the long‑term economics live.

One way to picture the difference:

- Training loads look like **spiky saw‑tooth curves**: big jobs, scheduled in batches, lots of idle time in between if you’re not careful.
- Mature inference looks more like a **flattened 24×7 line**: a constant stream of user requests, background agents, and periodic re‑ranking or planning.

In that world, two numbers start to dominate the discussion:

- **Cost per token** (or cost per action)  
- **SLOs**, especially **tail latency** (P95, P99)

In the *System Design* book and the *Computer Architecture* series, it’s natural to decompose this into two axes:

- **Throughput / concurrency** – how many tokens or actions the platform can process over a year.
- **Cost / SLO** – how much each token costs, and whether you can deliver it within the target latency band.

GTC 2026’s own framing lines up with this neatly: NVIDIA talks about the combined Blackwell + Rubin opportunity as roughly a **trillion‑dollar market before 2027**, and the interesting part is that most of that isn’t training. It’s long‑term, production‑grade inference.

You can summarize the contrast between training and inference in a simple table:

| Dimension           | Training                                      | Inference                                             |
|---------------------|-----------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| Workload pattern    | Episodic, batch jobs                          | Long‑lived, online, event‑driven                      |
| Resource usage      | Peaks during training runs, then idles or shifts | Always on; capacity is hard to “borrow” elsewhere  |
| Main KPIs           | Total training time, final model quality      | Cost per token / action, P95/P99 latency, SLO hit rate |
| Architecture focus  | Throughput and scaling efficiency for a single job | Multi‑tenant isolation, scheduling, and cost curves |

It’s also where the CPU’s new role becomes clear. If the CPU that orchestrates these requests is noisy, its jitter becomes tail latency at the system boundary. And once you promise external SLOs, tail latency is what your customers feel and pay for.

This is why Vera’s deterministic execution isn’t an isolated CPU feature. It’s one of the pillars of a broader architectural shift: **designing for sustained, production‑grade inference**, not one‑off training runs.

### 2.2 Prefill vs Decode Through the Lens of Arithmetic Intensity

To make this less abstract, let’s look at two phases of LLM inference through **Arithmetic Intensity (AI)**:

> **AI = FLOPs / Bytes**  
> (How many useful floating‑point operations you do per byte moved.)

In **training**, we usually think in epochs, datasets, and optimizer steps. In **inference**, especially at scale, it becomes natural to think in terms of **AI and SLOs**, and to realize that “inference” is not a single workload at all.

At a coarse level, you can split it into two very different phases:

| Phase       | Characteristics                            | Primary bottleneck | Typical optimization focus                       |
|------------|--------------------------------------------|--------------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| **Prefill**| Process the input prompt, build KV‑cache   | Memory‑ / bandwidth‑bound | HBM capacity / bandwidth, good pipelining   |
| **Decode** | Generate output token by token             | Latency‑ / control‑bound | KV‑cache locality, scheduler, micro‑kernels |

This split has a few immediate implications:

- Prefill tends to stream large amounts of weights and intermediate state into GPU memory; raw throughput matters more than single‑request latency.
- Decode behaves more like a real‑time system: a few extra milliseconds per token quickly turn into noticeable user wait time.
- At service scale, **overall cost and UX are a combination of both**; you can’t just optimize one phase in isolation.

The key trick is that by changing formats (NVFP4, 1.58‑bit) and layout (better KV cache organization, SoA vs AoS in some paths), you **increase AI** for the same logic:

- Same model, same answers, but fewer bytes moved per token.
- On the Roofline, you’ve nudged your workload to the right.

In practice, when you talk about disaggregated inference with infra teams, the conversation quickly turns into:

- How do we tune **AI** for each stage of the pipeline?  
- Which stages are GPU‑heavy vs CPU‑heavy vs network‑bound?
- Where do we pay state movement costs (KV, activations, metadata)?

Rubin, Vera, and the surrounding software stack are NVIDIA’s answer to that multi‑stage question.

### 2.3 Disaggregated Inference and the Role of DPU / Networking

Once you accept that inference is a pipeline, not a monolithic kernel, you can start asking: **what exactly are we disaggregating?**

At GTC 2026, NVIDIA’s story around NVL72 / NVL576, Spectrum‑X, and BlueField DPUs is essentially about three levers:

1. **Locality** 
   - Keep related work and state on the same tightly coupled GPU island when possible.
2. **Offload** 
   - Move networking, encryption, and some scheduling decisions off the host CPU.
3. **Queuing behavior** 
   - Use DPUs and smart NICs to prevent the interconnect from turning into a “queuing black hole.”

From a queuing perspective, you can think in terms of arrival rate **λ** and utilization **ρ**.

If you let ρ drift toward 1 on any critical link or service:

- Queue lengths explode
- Tail latency goes through the roof
- Retries and backoff make things worse, not better

BlueField‑class DPUs help here by acting as **traffic cops**:

- They terminate and shape flows closer to the edge of the cluster.
- They can enforce fairness and backpressure policies earlier.
- They keep the CPU and GPU pipeline from being overwhelmed by bursts.

GTC 2026’s Dynamo sessions [CWES81461] make this much more concrete by spelling out the main pieces in the stack:

| Component              | Role in the pipeline                                                  |
|------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Dynamo framework**   | Describes how to split and schedule Prefill vs Decode across clusters |
| **NIXL**               | A unified interconnect layer on top of RDMA, abstracting data moves   |
| **BlueField DPU**      | Offloads pacing, retransmits, and queue management from the host CPU  |
| **Multi‑tier KV‑cache**| Spreads KV‑cache across GPU HBM and DPU‑attached NVMe for huge models |

From a systems path point of view, a full disaggregated inference run looks roughly like this:

1. **Prefill @ Rubin / Blackwell clusters**  
   Large GPU clusters focus on high‑throughput KV‑cache generation: weights and intermediate features sit in HBM3e / HBM4; efficient GEMM / attention kernels turn long prompts into KV‑blocks.
2. **KV‑cache transfer via NIXL + RDMA**  
   NIXL handles connections, routing, and retry policy; BlueField offloads pacing and queue management so the GPUs see something that looks like “just another memory move,” not a thicket of network details.
3. **Decode @ decode nodes**  
   Latency‑sensitive decode runs on nodes closer to users or at least on a more tightly controlled topology, leaning on higher KV‑cache hit rates and better scheduling to keep tails in check.
4. **Multi‑tier KV‑cache for huge / MoE models**  
   Hot, recently used KV‑blocks stay in GPU HBM; colder ones spill to DPU‑managed local NVMe, which is still much closer than a distant storage tier.

This is especially important when **disaggregated inference** means:

- KV and intermediate state can live in different places (local, remote, tiered)
- Requests may be steered across multiple GPU islands
- Some parts of the pipeline may sit closer to the user (edge), others deep in the DC

In that world, your effective SLOs depend just as much on **queuing behavior in the fabric** as on FLOPs in the GPUs.

### 2.4 Software and Runtime: SGLang, vLLM, and Profiling the Pipeline

All of this hardware and networking work only pays off if the software stack is aware of it. A few of the projects highlighted at GTC 2026 were pushing in the same direction:

- **SGLang [CWES81934]** – Uses techniques like RadixAttention to share prefixes across requests, cutting redundant work in multi‑request batches.
- **vLLM [CWES82007]** – Uses PagedAttention and smarter KV‑cache placement to fit large models into the Blackwell / Vera memory hierarchy.
- **NeMo Agentic Toolkit [S81351]** – Provides long‑term memory, async task execution, and human‑in‑the‑loop (HITL) knowledge transfer for agentic systems.
- **Nsight Systems integration [DLIT81641]** – Gives high‑resolution profiling across the disaggregated inference pipeline, helping you spot bubbles from networking jitter or CPU orchestration delays.

These projects share a few assumptions:

- Inference is a **scheduling and resource‑management problem**, not just a kernel‑tuning problem.
- Frameworks need a first‑class notion of Prefill vs Decode so they can map them onto the right hardware and network paths.
- Engineers need end‑to‑end visibility: from “request arrives” to “token leaves,” with tooling to pinpoint where time and energy are actually spent.

In our own lab, this is where Li is usually in their element:

- They’ll grab Nsight Systems, trace the entire inference path, and redraw it on the whiteboard.
- Then they’ll line it up with the analysis methods from *Computer Architecture 04* to separate compute‑bound regions from memory‑bound ones and from pure scheduling / networking issues.

### 2.5 Scaling Out: USL and the Limits of "Just Add More Nodes"

When people first see slides about NVL576‑class systems, the natural instinct is:

> “If NVL72 is good, just bolt more of them together. How bad can scaling be?”

This is where the **Universal Scalability Law (USL)** gives you a useful dose of pessimism:

> **C(N) = N / [1 + σ (N − 1) + κ N (N − 1)]**

Where:

- **N** is the number of units (nodes, GPUs, racks, etc.)
- **σ** captures **contention** (how much time you spend waiting on shared resources)
- **κ** captures **coherency / consistency costs** (how much work you do to keep everything in sync)

Translated into the NVL72 → Kyber → NVL576 world:

- As you scale up, σ grows as more traffic contends for shared links, services, and metadata paths.
- κ grows as you introduce more cross‑node, cross‑rack communication and state sync.

NVIDIA’s networking and DPU story—NIXL, BlueField, Spectrum‑X—is largely about **pushing σ and κ down** so you get closer to linear scaling before USL’s “gravity” pulls you back.

In other words:

> **Disaggregated inference is not just 
“split the model across more boxes.” It’s an architectural strategy for negotiating with USL.**

It lets you decide, stage by stage:

- Which parts of the pipeline truly need global state
- Which parts can be sharded or replicated
- Where it’s worth paying cross‑rack penalties—and where it isn’t

This brings us to the next level up: not just scaling a cluster, but **designing an AI Factory** as a 20MW‑class physical site.

## 3. The AI Factory: Power, Capacity Planning, and the P‑E‑C Triangle

### 3.1 A 20MW Fermi Estimate: Making the Factory Physical

Before we start throwing numbers around, it helps to pin down what we even mean by an **AI Factory**.

From an engineering point of view, you can think of it as:

> **A factory that can reliably turn data into inference and action over long periods of time.**

At minimum, that factory spans several tightly coupled layers:

| Layer                 | Contents and representative technologies                                      |
|-----------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Compute**           | Vera CPU, Rubin GPU, and future LPUs / edge accelerators                      |
| **Networking**        | Spectrum‑X (Ethernet), Quantum‑X (InfiniBand), plus NIXL / RDMA / CPO on top  |
| **Storage / Data**    | High‑speed storage, data lakes, feature stores, multi‑tier KV‑cache           |
| **Software**          | NVIDIA NeMo, SGLang, vLLM, agentic toolkits, OpenClaw                         |
| **System Design**     | Power (800V DC), liquid cooling, rack layout, site selection and operations   |

With that picture on the board, Yang eventually closed the profiler and Lin opened a spreadsheet.

"If a customer says they have 20MW to play with," Lin asked, "roughly how many Rubin‑class GPUs are we talking about?"

We’re not trying to predict NVIDIA’s exact product specs here. Instead, we’ll walk through a **Fermi estimate** to turn 
"20MW AI site" into something you can picture.

> **Back‑of‑the‑envelope: Capacity Planning for a 20MW AI Site**  
> *(Numbers below are for teaching and order‑of‑magnitude intuition, not official specs.)*
>
> 1. **From site power to IT power**  
>    Assume PUE around 1.2. Out of 20MW at the fence, you get roughly 16MW for IT load.
> 2. **From IT power to racks**  
>    Assume ~120kW per rack for dense Rubin / NVL‑class systems. That gives you on the order of 130 racks.
> 3. **From racks to GPUs**  
>    Depending on how many NVL72 / NVL576 units per rack, you end up with something like **nine thousand Rubin GPUs** in a single site.

Again, the point is not the exact number. It’s to ground **“AI Factory”** in a physical image:

- Dozens to hundreds of high‑power racks
- Each carrying not just GPUs, but also DPUs, CPO‑based networking, and serious thermal infrastructure
- All operating inside a fixed **power and cooling envelope**

Once you see that, the architectural question changes from:

- “How do I get the best performance out of a single box?”

to:

- “Given 20MW and a PUE target, what’s the best way to tile this power across CPUs, GPUs, networking, and cooling so I hit my SLOs and my ROI window?”

### 3.2 The P‑E‑C Triangle and a CTO’s Decision Memo

In *System Design: An Architecture‑Aware Approach*, we often summarize capacity planning with a **P‑E‑C triangle**:

- **P – Performance**  
- **E – Energy / Power**  
- **C – Cost / Capex & Opex**

Any large‑scale deployment is essentially choosing a point inside this triangle. GTC 2026 puts concrete names and metrics on the vertices:

| Vertex               | Question space                                 | Example metrics                                                |
|----------------------|-----------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------|
| **P – Performance**  | How much inference capacity and how strict an SLO? | Tokens/s, concurrent sessions, e.g. **P99 < 100ms at 100k context** |
| **E – Energy**       | What power envelope are we willing to live in? | Site power (MW), PUE, kW per rack, joules per token            |
| **C – Cost**         | Is this site financially justifiable?         | \\$/token, annual TCO, payback period                          |

Within that triangle, pulling on any corner moves the other two:

- **P up** – you pack more NVL72 / Kyber / NVL576 capacity into the same site to chase higher throughput, but that pushes up both E (power) and C (capex / opex), and stresses networking and optics.
- **E tighter** – with a hard 20MW cap, you lean on aggressive numerical formats (NVFP4, 1.58‑bit), smarter scheduling, and disaggregated inference to do more work per watt; otherwise you have to relax P (SLOs, number of online models).
- **C down** – you stretch depreciation, raise utilization, or shift load to off‑peak power windows, but those choices bound what P and E you can realistically promise.

To make planning tractable, Lin treats NVL72 / Kyber / NVL576 as three different **building‑block scales** inside the triangle:

| Scale          | What it roughly represents                                                             |
|----------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **NVL72**      | 72 Rubin GPUs + 36 Vera CPUs in a single liquid‑cooled rack; NVLink 6 over short copper |
| **Kyber / NVL144** | Next‑gen MGX designs: expand the single‑rack NVLink domain to 144 Rubin GPUs, pushing 400G SerDes and copper to their practical limits |
| **NVL576**     | A 576‑GPU NVLink domain built from multiple NVL72 / Kyber racks; cross‑rack links lean heavily on optics and CPO |

Viewed through P‑E‑C:

- From **P**’s side, you’d love to fit as much work as possible into a big NVL576 domain to minimize cross‑domain traffic.
- From **E**’s side, NVL576‑class systems mean higher per‑site power density and tougher cooling, forcing you to reshuffle the 20MW power and cooling budget.
- From **C**’s side, those decisions flow straight into cost per token and site‑level TCO—exactly why GTC 2026 frames the AI Factory as a financial construct as much as a technical one.

Lin, looking at the triangle on the board, posed three uncomfortable but realistic questions—the sort that would show up in a CTO’s notebook, not a marketing slide:

1. **Power vs Compute Headroom**  
   If we pay the premium for more aggressive liquid cooling and lower PUE, how much extra compute do we get over a 3–5 year window, and does that amortize the upfront cost?
2. **Scale‑up vs Scale‑out**  
   Do our current inference workloads really need NVL576‑class cross‑rack fabrics, or can we get away with multiple quasi‑independent NVL72 “cells” for now?
3. **Time‑to‑market vs Future‑proofing**  
   Given our power and construction timelines, do we deploy “slightly older, available‑now” architectures, or wait for something with a better P‑E‑C point that ships later?

The P‑E‑C triangle turns these from vague strategy questions into something you can actually sketch, quantify, and argue about on a whiteboard.

### 3.3 “Electricity with a Timetable”

One of the more memorable phrases around GTC 2026 was the idea of AI Factories as **“electricity with a timetable.”**

It captures a simple but powerful shift:

- You’re no longer selling “GPU boxes.”
- You’re selling the ability to **deliver specific SLOs**—tokens, actions, or tasks—within a fixed power envelope, on a fixed schedule.

When you connect that to Spectrum‑X / Quantum‑X and CPO‑based networking, the picture closes:

- Without more efficient, denser networking, you simply can’t wire up enough NVL‑class systems inside 20MW to hit the desired P‑E‑C point.
- Without good thermal control for MRMs and optics, your SLOs will erode under jitter, retries, and fabric‑level tail latency.

So when we say an AI Factory is “electricity with a timetable,” what we’re really saying is:

> **You are selling the ability to deliver a certain amount of reliable inference and action inside a fixed power and time envelope.**

Seen from the industry’s value chain, AI Factories effectively re‑cut the stack into a few layers:

1. **Chips and core compute** – vendors building GPUs, CPUs, DPUs, LPUs, and other accelerators.
2. **AI cloud and GPU services** – providers offering GPU‑as‑a‑Service and managed AI platforms.
3. **Physical infrastructure** – specialists who design and operate high‑power, high‑density data centers.
4. **Power and power electronics** – generation, transmission, and the power‑electronics ecosystem that makes 20MW‑class sites viable.
5. **AI‑native companies and applications** – businesses whose core product is built around AI (from foundation‑model labs to industry‑specific platforms).

The point is not to memorize which vendor sits where, but to keep the mental model straight:

> **When you talk about an AI Factory, what’s actually being bought and sold is the ability to deliver trustworthy inference and action at a given time, in a given power envelope.**

From here, the next question is inevitable: what changes when those inferences and actions **leave the data center** and start moving robots, machines, and vehicles in the physical world?

## 4. Physical AI: Extending from Cloud to the Real World

### 4.1 From Neat Demos to Safety‑Critical Systems

Later that evening, we replayed a few of NVIDIA’s robotics demos in the lab.

After the third loop of a humanoid stumbling and standing back up, Chen finally asked:

“This looks cool, but how is this really different from what we’ve been talking about with GPUs and NVLink? Isn’t it just the same stack with a walking shell?”

I froze the video at the frame where the robot was lifting its foot.

“Up to this frame,” I said, “the first three themes we talked about—Vera, disaggregated inference, AI Factories—are enough. Starting from this frame, you’re officially in **Physical AI** territory.”

In GTC 2026 materials and demos, you can roughly group Physical AI into three buckets:

- **Robotics / Humanoids** – training robots to walk, manipulate, and collaborate via Isaac Lab, GR00T, and related tools.
- **Autonomous Machines / Vehicles** – from warehouses to vehicles, combining perception, planning, and control loops with AI models.
- **Industrial Digital Twins** – full‑physics simulations and AI driving things like tsunami warning systems and factory twins.

The common thread is straightforward:

- AI outputs map **directly** to actions and control signals in the physical world.
- Latency and determinism requirements go up; the cost of errors now includes equipment damage and safety incidents, not just bad UX.

In our lab, this is usually when Wang—the one responsible for system security and isolation—chimes in:

> “If this thing eventually goes on the road or into a factory, which failure modes turn directly into safety hazards? In the cloud, worst case, you reload the web page. In Physical AI, a tail latency spike could mean a robot arm punching through a production line. How do we convince a certification body that the **worst‑case execution time** of this stack is under control?”

That’s the crux of the difference:

> **Physical AI is not “cloud LLMs with a robot skin on top.” It drags everything we’ve discussed about determinism, queuing, and jitter into a safety‑critical world.**

Vera’s deterministic CPU design, disaggregated inference across edge and cloud, and the 20MW AI Factory envelope now all have to reconcile with **functional safety** requirements and long‑term operability.

### 4.2 Edge, Cloud, and the Software–Hardware Alignment

Compared to pure cloud inference, Physical AI raises the bar in a few specific ways:

- **Ultra‑low latency and tight jitter** in control loops
- **Hybrid edge + cloud architectures** – some inference and control must run on‑device or near the edge, with the cloud handling long‑horizon planning and model updates
- **Safety and certification** – deployment pipelines need to satisfy functional safety standards, not just A/B testing requirements

From an architectural point of view, this reinforces earlier themes and reframes Vera’s role:

- Vera’s deterministic execution is not just about cost; it’s about meeting functional safety and long‑term maintainability requirements in real‑time control loops.
- Disaggregated inference thinking now extends to questions like: which parts of the policy and control logic stay on the robot or vehicle, and which run in the cloud?
- The AI Factory’s power and network design determines how often and how reliably edge devices can sync state and fetch updated models.

If we translate these constraints into software‑stack language, an interesting pattern emerges:

> **NVIDIA isn’t building OpenClaw and NeMo because it wants to become a pure software company. It’s because this software has to “grow” on top of hardware like Vera and Rubin to turn determinism and compute into real‑world behavior.**

A rough mapping might look like this:

| Software Layer / Tooling              | Primary Role                                | Hardware / System Counterpart                                      |
|--------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **OpenClaw Reasoning Engine**       | Task decomposition, agent coordination      | **Vera CPU** – deterministic orchestrator for control loops        |
| **OpenClaw Action Layer**           | Tool calls, actuation, simulation, robots  | **Rubin / NVL72 / NVL576** – GPU pools for large‑model inference   |
| **NeMo / Safety Sandboxes / Guards**| Permissions, safety checks, isolation       | **OS / Hypervisor / Isolation (MIG, SR‑IOV, etc.)**                |

The precise product names and integrations will evolve. The key is the **mental model**: when you design a Physical AI system, your reasoning, action, and sandbox layers must each line up with concrete hardware and system guarantees.

### 4.3 Engineer’s Note: CPO, MRMs, and Thermal Control Loops

Back in Section 1.5, we briefly touched on CPO and MRMs as enablers of high‑density, power‑efficient networking.

From an engineer’s perspective, you can think of MRMs as:

- Tiny resonant structures tuned to a specific wavelength
- Amazing when you keep them on target
- Painful when thermal drift pulls them away

In practice:

- Small temperature swings can degrade loss and eye quality across a whole bank of channels.
- To compensate, you may be forced to retune, slow down, or retransmit.

This ties several earlier sections together:

- In **Section 1.5**, we saw why Rubin‑class power density and CPO thermal budgets push you toward 800V + liquid cooling and tightly controlled rack thermals.
- In **Section 3.1**, those same CPO / MRM requirements were implicitly baked into our per‑rack power assumptions and the overall 20MW site envelope.
- In **Section 2**, we saw how jitter and retransmits in the network can show up as tail latency and failed SLOs at the application edge.

The punchline for most engineers isn’t that you need to memorize photonics textbooks. It’s this:

> **The deeper you go into AI Factories and Physical AI, the more “packaging” and “thermal” details show up as queuing, jitter, and reliability issues at the system level.**

The thermal control loops keeping MRMs inside a safe operating band are, in a very real sense, part of your system’s latency and reliability budget.

## 5. Conclusion: What the AI Factory Era Demands from Us

Looking back at GTC 2026, we can summarize the shift along three axes:

1. **From training to inference** – inference becomes the main business, and infrastructure design follows.
2. **From thinking to acting** – agentic AI requires new CPUs, runtimes, and system designs to support long‑running tasks and multi‑agent collaboration.
3. **From digital to physical** – Physical AI extends the same infrastructure into robots, factories, and safety‑critical systems.

Under these shifts, a handful of technologies tie the story together:

- **Vera CPU** – deterministic execution and low‑latency orchestration, so large numbers of agents can run in a stable, predictable environment.
- **Rubin GPU + HBM4** – pushing memory bandwidth and capacity forward, making larger models and longer context feasible.
- **Disaggregated inference** – decomposing the inference pipeline so each stage runs on the most appropriate hardware and software.
- **AI Factories** – treating compute, networking, software, storage, power, and cooling as a single design and procurement unit.

From a methods perspective, this article really used a small set of simple models to put everything on one whiteboard:

- **Roofline / Arithmetic Intensity** – to understand 
  “compute vs bandwidth” and why formats like NVFP4 and 1.58‑bit can move the bottleneck.
- **Little’s Law and queuing theory** – to reason about tail latency and determinism, and why Vera’s design matters for agentic workloads.
- **USL (Universal Scalability Law)** – to frame how NVL72 → Kyber → NVL576 scale‑out behaves and why cross‑rack communication eventually hits diminishing returns.
- **P‑E‑C Triangle and “electricity with a timetable”** – to project all of these choices into the 20MW AI Factory’s power and financial envelope.

For engineers, this implies a few concrete takeaways:

- We need to cultivate **system‑level thinking**, not just kernel‑level or component‑level optimization.
- We need to understand **disaggregated architectures**, and how to split and schedule workloads across heterogeneous clusters.
- We need to care deeply about **deterministic execution and latency distributions**, because agentic and Physical AI systems are unusually sensitive to tail behavior.

For organizations and decision‑makers, the AI Factory era brings another layer of meaning:

- Buying decisions shift from “how many GPUs” to “how do we design an AI site under a 20MW / PUE constraint,” which is really a P‑E‑C decision.
- Vendor and solution evaluation must consider **power, speed, resilience, and proof of delivery**, not just headline FLOPs or token/s.
- The durable differentiator is less about any single chip and more about who can offer a **verifiable path to production** within given power and time budgets.

At the end of that long evening in the lab, as the whiteboard filled with circles, arrows, and formulas, I found myself quietly asking my students the same question I now leave with you:

> **In an era where AI Factories are taking shape, is the layer you’re responsible for ready?**
